# Topic 08: Go-To-Market for Scientific Customers

## Overview
Pharma/biotech sales motion, KOL engagement, scientific credibility building, and adoption dynamics unique to selling AI tools to scientists and clinicians.

---

### Q1: How does the sales cycle and buying process for a BioAI discovery platform differ from typical enterprise SaaS, and what does this mean for product/GTM sequencing?

**A:**
**Key differences:**
1. **Multi-stakeholder, credibility-gated buying process:** Purchase decisions typically require both a bench-scientist champion (who must be convinced the tool produces scientifically credible results, often via hands-on pilot evaluation against their own data) and a budget-holding executive — winning the scientist's trust is usually the binding constraint, not the economic buyer's approval, inverting the typical enterprise SaaS pattern where economic buyer alignment is often the harder win
2. **Long, evidence-heavy sales cycles:** Pharma/biotech customers often require extensive pilot/validation periods (sometimes 6-12+ months) against their own proprietary data before committing, since a wrong tool choice has real scientific and financial consequences (wasted wet-lab budget chasing bad AI-suggested candidates) — this is fundamentally different from typical SaaS trial-to-paid conversion timelines
3. **Publication/peer-review as a credibility currency:** Peer-reviewed publications, preprints, and conference presentations (e.g., at AACR, NeurIPS-MLSB workshops) function as a de facto sales/marketing channel in a way with no clean analogue in typical enterprise software — scientific credibility is earned through the same channels scientists use to evaluate each other's work, not through traditional marketing collateral
4. **Reference customers carry outsized weight, but are hard to obtain:** A named, credible pharma/biotech reference customer significantly accelerates subsequent sales, but early customers are often reluctant to be named publicly (competitive sensitivity about their AI tool usage) — securing referenceable customers is a deliberate, often incentivized GTM strategy question, not an automatic byproduct of successful deployments

**Product/GTM sequencing implications:**
- Roadmap should prioritize enabling credible, well-instrumented pilot evaluations (e.g., built-in benchmarking against a customer's own historical data) as a core product capability, not an afterthought — since the pilot IS the primary sales conversion mechanism
- Early product investment in publication-worthy validation studies (in partnership with early customers or via public benchmarks) can be a legitimate, high-ROI GTM investment, distinct from traditional demand-gen spending
- GTM timeline expectations (and associated revenue forecasting) must account for realistically long pilot-to-contract cycles rather than importing typical SaaS sales cycle assumptions

### Q2: How would you build credibility with a skeptical scientific/clinical customer base for a new BioAI product category where trust in AI is still being established?

**A:**
**Credibility-building framework:**
1. **Lead with rigorous, honest validation data — including limitations:** Skeptical scientific audiences respond better to transparent reporting of where a model works well and where it doesn't (with appropriate statistical rigor — confidence intervals, not just point estimates) than to marketing claims that oversell capability; overclaiming that later proves wrong in a customer's hands is far more damaging in scientific communities (which talk to each other) than a competitor's superior initial marketing
2. **Publish/present in venues the target customer actually trusts:** Peer-reviewed journals and respected conferences carry more weight with scientific buyers than company blog posts or press releases — GTM strategy should include a deliberate scientific publication/conference presence plan, coordinated between product, science, and marketing functions
3. **Enable independent verification wherever possible:** Offering benchmark results on public, independently-checkable datasets (in addition to any proprietary validation) allows skeptical customers to verify claims themselves rather than relying purely on vendor-reported numbers — this transparency is itself a trust-building product/GTM decision
4. **Recruit credible scientific advisors/KOLs (Key Opinion Leaders) genuinely, not just for logo value:** A respected domain scientist's genuine, substantive engagement (co-authored validation studies, honest public commentary) carries far more credibility than a superficial "advisory board" relationship — PMs and GTM leaders should prioritize depth of engagement over breadth of advisor logos
5. **Design the product's first real-world use cases to be verifiable/falsifiable, not vague:** Early customer success stories framed around concrete, checkable claims ("model-prioritized candidates showed a 3x hit-rate improvement over the customer's prior random/heuristic selection in this specific campaign") build more durable credibility than vague "accelerated our discovery process" testimonials that skeptics can dismiss as unverifiable

### Q3–Q15: (Representative additional topics)
- Freemium/open-access strategies for building scientific community trust and adoption (e.g., releasing model weights or a limited free tier)
- Navigating IT security/data governance review processes at large pharma/health system customers
- Pricing model design for BioAI products (per-seat, per-prediction, outcome-based, platform licensing)
- Managing the tension between publishing scientific validation (credibility) and protecting competitive IP
- Building GTM motion for platform products serving both academic and commercial (pharma/biotech) customers
- Channel/partnership strategy (e.g., partnering with CROs, cloud providers, instrument vendors)
- Customer success/onboarding design for scientifically sophisticated but software-unsophisticated users
- Positioning against both "AI-native" competitors and incumbent non-AI tools in RFP processes
- Navigating procurement processes unique to academic institutions vs. commercial biotech/pharma
- Conference/trade show strategy specific to scientific and clinical audiences (AACR, JPM Healthcare, HIMSS, NeurIPS)

---

## Summary
Go-to-market for BioAI products requires treating scientific credibility as the primary conversion currency — pilots, publications, and transparent validation data function as the core sales motion in a way that has no direct equivalent in typical enterprise software go-to-market playbooks.
